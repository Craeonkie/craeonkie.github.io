A variety of different Arthropods live on this planet. Here are the 4 most commonly known groups.

Arachnids

Arachnids are a class of arthropods that include spiders, scorpions, ticks, and mites. They are characterized by having eight legs, no antennae, and bodies divided into two main segments: the cephalothorax and abdomen. Unlike insects, arachnids do not have wings or compound eyes. They are predominantly terrestrial and are known for their adaptations to a wide variety of environments, from deserts to forests. Arachnids are thought to have evolved from marine ancestors, likely close relatives of horseshoe crabs, during the Silurian period over 430 million years ago. As they adapted to land, they developed features like book lungs or tracheae for breathing air, and many evolved venom for predation or defense.

Crustaceans

Crustaceans are a diverse group of primarily aquatic arthropods that include crabs, lobsters, shrimp, barnacles, and copepods. They usually have hard exoskeletons reinforced with calcium, two pairs of antennae, and specialized appendages for swimming, feeding, and walking. Crustaceans are especially dominant in marine environments and are vital parts of aquatic food chains. They likely evolved from a common ancestor shared with insects and myriapods during the Cambrian period, roughly 500 million years ago. Their early evolutionary adaptations allowed them to thrive in water, including the development of gills for underwater respiration and complex larval stages that aid in dispersal and survival.

Hexapods

Hexapods, the group that includes insects, are the most numerous and diverse class of arthropods. They are defined by having six legs, three distinct body segments (head, thorax, and abdomen), and often wings. Insects like beetles, butterflies, and ants have colonized nearly every terrestrial habitat on Earth. Hexapods are believed to have originated from a crustacean-like ancestor, which transitioned to land during the Devonian period over 400 million years ago. Their evolution of wings and compact body plans gave them a massive evolutionary advantage, allowing them to explore the air and develop highly specialized ecological roles, from pollinators to decomposers.

Myriapods

Myriapods include centipedes and millipedes, and are known for their long, segmented bodies and many legs—millipedes can have hundreds! They are entirely terrestrial and mostly dwell in moist environments, where they feed on decaying plant material (in the case of millipedes) or are active predators (in the case of centipedes). Myriapods are among the earliest arthropods to colonize land, appearing around 420 million years ago. Their ancestors likely diverged from the same lineage as insects and crustaceans, but evolved in isolation as they adapted to the soil and leaf litter environments, developing strong exoskeletons and breathing through spiracles and tracheal systems.

Take a look at all the different places Arthropods have been across our culture and media!

Arachne and the Loom of Fate

In Greek mythology, Arachne was a mortal weaver whose hubris led her to challenge the goddess Athena. After besting the goddess in a weaving contest, Arachne was transformed into a spider—marking one of the earliest tales connecting humans to arachnids. Her story is both a cautionary tale and a symbolic origin for our modern word “arachnid,” cementing the spider as a figure of skill, pride, and divine punishment in Western mythos.

Charlotte’s Web: The Kindness of a Spider

Charlotte, the spider from E.B. White’s *Charlotte's Web*, is one of the most beloved arthropods in children's literature. Her intelligence, creativity, and compassion help save Wilbur the pig by weaving persuasive messages into her web. Charlotte challenges the traditional fear of spiders by portraying one as wise, articulate, and nurturing, forever changing how many children perceive these eight-legged creatures.

Ants: The Underdogs of Animation

From Pixar’s *A Bug’s Life* to DreamWorks’ *Antz*, ants have been portrayed as resilient and clever creatures that thrive through cooperation. These films anthropomorphize ant colonies to explore social themes like individuality, hierarchy, and rebellion. Their industrious nature and role as tiny heroes make them ideal protagonists in tales of perseverance.

Bees: Buzzing Between Comedy and Crisis

Bees in media oscillate between comic relief and ecological ambassadors. *The Bee Movie* gave us the bizarre yet memorable tale of a bee suing humanity, while documentaries and news stories stress their vital role in pollination and the growing concerns of colony collapse. Their cultural footprint spans humor, activism, and admiration.

Scarab Beetles: Symbols of Eternal Life

Revered in ancient Egypt, scarab beetles were seen as symbols of rebirth and the rising sun. Depicted frequently in amulets and tomb carvings, they were believed to guide souls through the afterlife. Their spiritual importance persists, making them one of the most iconic mythological arthropods in human history.

Mothra: Guardian of Nature

A giant moth kaiju from the Godzilla franchise, Mothra stands out as both a destructive force and a divine protector. Often portrayed with themes of sacrifice and balance, Mothra has gained a reputation as a symbol of nature’s power, beauty, and wrath—contrasting the typical monstrous depiction of insects in cinema.

Crab Rave: Arthropods in Internet Culture

*Crab Rave*, a viral music video and meme by Noisestorm, turned dancing crabs into symbols of ironic celebration. Often used to signal humorous 'good riddance' events, it represents a modern, digital age fascination with arthropods—now not feared or revered, but gleefully memed.

Silkworms and the Birth of Chinese Silk

The humble silkworm revolutionized history through its role in silk production, central to ancient Chinese economy and culture. Legend tells of Empress Leizu discovering silk while sipping tea beneath a mulberry tree. These arthropods helped shape trade routes and luxury markets through the famous Silk Road.

Fleas and the Black Death

Fleas—tiny arthropods often overlooked—played a devastating role in history as vectors of the Black Death. Transmitted via infected rats and fleas, the plague reshaped Europe, leading to widespread mortality, economic collapse, and long-term societal changes. Their legacy is a grim reminder of arthropods' capacity to impact civilization.

Arthropods make up the largest and most diverse group of animals on Earth, so it is no surprise that humans interact with them in vastly different manners.

Crustaceans, our favourite delicacy

Crustaceans, such as crabs, lobsters, shrimp, and prawns, are widely consumed across the globe and often considered delicacies. They're likely what comes to mind when you hear the concept of eating Arthropods.

Found in both marine and freshwater environments, they’re staples in cuisines from East Asia to the Mediterranean. Their rich, meaty texture and flavor make them central to countless traditional dishes, and their high protein content adds to their appeal.

Chili Crab, one of Singapore's many signature dishes

Insects can be eaten?!?

Insects, although less common in Western diets, are widely eaten in parts of Asia, Africa, and Central and South America. Species like crickets, grasshoppers, and mealworms are rich in protein, vitamins, and minerals, and are often roasted, fried, or ground into flour. As sustainable food sources, insects are gaining global attention for their low environmental impact compared to traditional livestock.

8 limbs of pure protein

Arachnids, like scorpions and tarantulas, are more niche but still consumed in various cultures. In some parts of Southeast Asia, deep-fried tarantulas are a street food attraction, valued both for their novelty and crunchy texture. Scorpions, too, are eaten in similar fashion, sometimes even skewered and served whole.

Healing Bugs

Myriapods, such as centipedes, are rarely eaten but can still be found in some regional dishes or medicinal preparations, particularly in parts of China. Their strong flavor and unusual appearance make them a far less common choice, but they are still a part of the broader conversation on edible arthropods. Some practicecs put them in jars of alchohol to ferment, and are claimed to have medicinal properties.

So, why are some Arthropods, namely crustaceans, are regarded as a delicacy world wide, while most others are shunned and avoided?

There are many reasons, but the most likely reasons are that:

  1. Crustaceans, the most commonly eaten, are usually found in water, a complately different world from us, whereas most of the other arthropods live on land like us.

  2. Due to their water environment, Crustaceans are usually slow and not threathening looking. They are also bigger, making them much easier for humans to eat them. Whereas with many of the other arthropods, they live on land, flying around and crawling very fast with their small bodies, which scares us.

  3. Many crustaceans are not poisonous, whereas most of the land arthropods commonly are either venomous or poisonous, discouraging us from eating them.

  4. The small size of the other arthropods make it not worth dissecting them like we do for crabs and prawns and lobsters, resulting in us eating them whole should we try to, their tough outer chitin making them taste bitter. However, many insects, without their shells, actually tend to taste similar to our familiar shellfish!
